The aim of this experiment is to examine how people are able to make decisions from very briefly presented visual information. In particular, we are interested in how the limitations on attention and memory might prevent people from making accurate decisions when the number and locations of potentially important pieces of information changes. Although we often can feel like we see the world as a relatively continuous, fluid experience, however that experience is limited by the speed by which we can process the incoming visual information and capacity of our brain to operate on that information.

Some of the trials in this task were easier than others: you would have noticed that the number of items changed from one trial to the next and you may have perhaps noticed that some trials were faster than others. By selecting different types of trials which require different levels of processing, and by controlling the amount of information available to you, we are able to examine the very limits of perception through examining both the number of errors that you made but also the speed at which you were able to respond.

Although the experiment can be a little repetitive, we hope that in undertaking the experiment, you were able to reflect on the limits of perception and the way that, even in a very simple task, those limits may allow us to understand something about how the brain performs the complicated task of piecing together a complete picture of the world in everyday life.
